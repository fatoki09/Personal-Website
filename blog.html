A Royal Heartbreak: My Journey as a Sacramento Kings Fan

Written by Ramon Flores on January 11, 2013 at 5:00 PM.

When I was only six years old, my family took me to see my first ever Kings game. It was game 4 of the first round of the playoffs and the Kings we're playing John Stockton, Karl Malone, and the Utah Jazz. The Kings were the new kids on the block, with rising stars Jason Williams and Peja Stojakovic and the best big man duo of Chris Webber and Vlade Divac. I didn't know much about basketball, but the atmosphere at the stadium was incredible. Cowbells were ringing everywhere and we got so loud that Utah coach Jerry Sloan even had to wear earplugs. We ended up losing the game on a Stockton three, but this single game sparked in me a genuine love for basketball that continues to this day. This marked a golden age for the Kings and I will never forget Jason Williams' no look passes and Vlade blowing kisses to the crowd.

The next few years were the best years of my life as a sports fan. The Kings were a perennial power and championship contenders. We traded J-Will for Mike Bibby and signed ultra-defender Doug Christie as well as future sixth man Bobby Jackson. It was the perfect team. Sports Illustrated even called us "The Greatest Show on Court," because the Kings showed exactly how basketball is supposed to be played. Our offense was beautiful -- players were always moving and nearly every basket was an assisted one. In 2002, we were the heavy favorites to win the championship until the referees rigged game 6 and we lost the series to the hated Lakers. I thought we would win again the following year, but Chris Webber tore his ACL. I didn't realize it at the time, but that was the end of the Kings glory days. C-Webb came back towards the end of the next season, but he wasn't the same. He couldn't make explosive moves to the basketball nor get rebounds over two or three people. Regardless, we were a three-pointer away from making the Western Conference Finals but still came up short.

We made the playoffs a couple of years after that, but for the most part being a Kings fan has been disappointing and frustrating up to this point. We never seemed to make the right moves from then on and our players never seemed to reach their full potential. We haven't even been close to making the playoffs since 2006. Just a couple of days ago, I heard some news that was more heartbreaking than the past six or seven season of losing: Seattle investors were negotiating with the Maloofs (owners of the Kings) to relocate the team to Seattle.

I was on my way to class when I heard this and I couldn't help but check Twitter the entire class to see all of the updates. I reminisced on all of the memories I've had as a Kings fan and how thankful I am to have grown up with Mike Bibby, Peja Stojakovic, Chris Webber, and Vlade Divac. I can remember Chris Webber knocking down John Stockton in 1999, Mike Bibby's game winner against the Lakers in 2002, Peja Stojakovic's game-tying three against the T-Wolves in 2004, and Kevin Martin's buzzer beater against the Spurs in 2006. I can remember them as clear as day. The thing is, whether or not the Kings were the best or the worst team in the league, I've loved them the same. I've always had the same passion for them. I will always come to my computer or TV when the Kings are playing and I will always get nervous when they are in a close game. TNT's Kenny Smith said it best, "When we were winning or we were losing, it was always the same in Sacramento." Regardless of who are players are, I will always support the team and come October I am always looking forward to a new season of Sacramento basketball. It would truly be a travesty to have the Kings leave such a great city and such a LOYAL and DEVOTED fanbase.

Honestly speaking, the Kings have the best fanbase in the NBA. Of the 19 seasons where we've sold out EVERY game, only 10 of those season were we a playoff team. We have the most loyal fans in the NBA. We have the record for most consecutive sellouts by an NBA franchise and formerly held the world record for loudest sports roar. I can't think of another fanbase that makes other teams wear earplugs during their games. I can't think of other arenas where players can't hear their coaches calling out a play or calling a timeout. You see, Sacramento isn't a big city like LA or San Francisco. It has a much more intimate environment and everyone comes out to the games to support together as a community. It's not just about the team, it's about the city. No other NBA city can replicate the dedication and basketball atmosphere we have here in Sacramento.

Do I feel for Seattle for losing the SuperSonics? Yeah, I do. But at the same time I do realize that the Sonics never meant as much to them as the Kings do to Sacramentans. Seattle has the Mariners, the Sounders, and the Seahawks who are currently in the playoffs. They never loved Gary Payton and Ray Allen as much as they did Matt Hasselbeck and Shaun Alexander. When Chris Webber was traded to Sacramento, he outwardly spoke of how he did not want to be there. We still welcomed him with open arms and even had a city wide celebration when he came to the city and even when he returned from his ACL injury. He now loves the city and still has a restaurant there. In fact, I can see his jersey hanging from the rafters whenever I come to a game. There's nothing like basketball in Sacramento and I'm truly praying that Sacramento will beat the offers from Seattle firms and keep the Kings where they truly belong -- the place where they will be loved by the most loyal fans in the sport. Hopefully one day, I will be able to celebrate an NBA championships with my favorite team in all of sports in my hometown of Sacramento.

Winter Quarter 2013

Written by Ramon Flores on January 8, 2013 at 12:16 AM.

So I've had a couple of days to settle back into college life again, so I thought I'd write a post about my classes and what I want to do this quarter. I'm taking 16 units this quarter from 4 CS classes. I've never taken more than 2 computer science classes in a single quarter, so I will have to adjust somewhat to being on the computer literally the entire day. At the same time, I'm going to learn so much this quarter and I can't wait.

My first class is CS 110: systems. The first lecture seemed a bit dry, but knowing about the hardware and exactly how computers run seems to be a valuable skill and I've heard the programming assignments are very good as well. My second class is CS 103: logic. My favorite professor, Keith Schwarz is teaching the class and his first lecture was funny and awesome in general. I haven't taken a math class in over a year but this one seems to be a good one. More on my other two classes later because they begin tomorrow.

In terms of this quarter, I want to be more efficient and productive with my time in general. I'm hoping to sleep a little earlier (1-2 AM rather than 3-4 AM) so I can feel energized in the morning. I'm hoping to swim a mile to a mile and a half every morning before class, play some basketball in the evenings, and eat much healthier in general. I also want to be more efficient when I am doing homework without distractions such as ESPN, facebook, or FIFA 13. Most of all, I hope to interview well at tech companies and land a software engineering internship this summer. I'd love the opportunity to work for a company and gain industry experience while developing as a programmer. Lastly, I want to explore more. There's so much more Stanford has to offer that I haven't seen yet. I hope to visit some more of the frats and houses on the row and check out more of the surrounding areas -- Palo Alto, Mountain View, San Jose.

I'm excited to come back to a fresh quarter and I'm ready to make more memories with all of my friends.

2013 Rose Bowl

Written by Ramon Flores on January 2, 2013 at 11:15 PM.

I spent New Year's Day with my dad in Pasadena to watch my Stanford Cardinal take on the Wisconsin Badgers in the 99th Rose Bowl Game presented by Vizio. Needless to say, I couldn't have kicked off 2013 any better. On September 16th, I tweeted that Stanford was going to the Rose Bowl and they in fact did, proving hundreds of experts wrong. Our team lost star Andrew Luck to the NFL and many thought we would not be nearly as good, but out defense really stepped up and proved to be one of the best in the nation.

So back to the Rose Bowl. At 6 AM on New Year's Day, my dad and I left to drive over to Pasadena. We stopped at Burger King for some breakfast and began our five hour journey. Along the way, we stopped by for some gas and some snacks. We knew we were close when there was traffic backed up in the freeway. It took us about an hour to travel the two miles from the freeway exit to the actual parking area, which was on an 18-hole golf course.

Once we parked, we could see the vast array of red and white from Stanford and Wisconsin fans alike. Fans from both schools tailgated amongst each other and I had to take a second look to see if certain people were from Wisconsin or Stanford. We then walked around the entire arena to get our tickets, which by the way were the most beautiful looking tickets I have ever seen. After eating a not so delicious burrito and a cinnamon roll, it was finally time for the game.

Stanford got off to a 14-0 start and my dad and I thought it was going to be a blowout. But their Doak Walker Award runningback Montee Ball willed them to two touchdowns. 17-14 Stanford at halftime. The third quarter was a weird one -- with no points and Stanford not even getting a single first down. Tense moments for my dad and I because we could have lost the lead at any moment. Stanford finally cured the scoring drought in the fourth with a field goal to make it 20-14. With about 4 minutes left, Wisconsin had the chance to win with a touchdown but was intercepted by Stanford nickelback Usua Amanam, and the rest was history.

We drove back that same night and got home at about 1:30 AM. I can honestly say that this has been the best sports experience I have ever had. It was a great game that went down to the wire and I will always remember 40,000+ Stanford fans screaming in delight at the game-winning interception. It wasn't just the game, it was the tradition and significance of the Rose Bowl itself -- the nearly 100,000 fans, the tailgate, and the atmosphere in general. I'll always remember this most amazing New Year's Day and here's to the Cardinal making the national championship next year. Hopefully I'll be back in Pasadena again.
